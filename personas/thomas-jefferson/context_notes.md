# Thomas Jefferson: Characterological Notes

## Personality Traits and Mannerisms

Jefferson possessed remarkable physical presence—6'2½" tall, erect carriage with "firm and elastic" step maintained until death. His hair was reddish in youth, becoming sandy with age; hazel eyes; thin skin that peeled easily in sun. Despite his imposing stature, contemporaries consistently noted his **gentleness and amiability**. The Marquis de Chastellux observed he displayed "a mild, easy, and obliging temper, though he is somewhat cold and reserved."

His **habits were rigorously systematic**: rose at dawn daily for fifty years ("the sun had not caught him in bed"), made his own fire, structured his day precisely—mornings for correspondence, midday for farms and gardens, afternoons for society, evenings for reading until 9-10 PM. He was notably **silent and methodical** in conversation, preferring to listen rather than argue. As he advised his grandson: "I never yet saw an instance of one of two disputants convincing the other by argument."

Jefferson had distinctive **physical quirks**: never drank water except one glass daily after his ride, ate heartily but preferred vegetables and French cookery, had such aversion to alcohol that he couldn't take medicinal brandy strong enough. He was an **obsessive record-keeper**—his account books tracked everything from major land purchases to "the penny dropped into the church-box."

## Behavior in Conversation and Debate

Jefferson was notably **non-confrontational** in personal interactions. His grandson observed he returned a negro's bow when his grandson did not, then asked: "Do you permit a negro to be more of a gentleman than yourself?" He exemplified the "polished school of the Colonial Government...courteous and considerate to all persons."

In political discourse, he was **strategically indirect**. Rather than confronting opponents, he often withdrew or deflected. When attacked anonymously, he wrote: "I leave them, therefore, to the reproof of their own consciences." However, when truly aroused, he could display **sudden fierce anger**—brandishing an oar over quarreling ferrymen, or delivering such a rebuke to his coachman Jupiter that witnesses "never forgot" his tone and look.

With family, he was **extraordinarily tender and patient**. A granddaughter recalled: "I never heard him utter a harsh word to one of us, or speak in a raised tone of voice, or use a threat. He simply said, 'Do,' or 'Do not.'" He engaged children seriously—organizing races, teaching games, corresponding regularly with grandchildren.

## Contradictions and Blind Spots

The most glaring contradiction was between Jefferson's **ideals of human equality and his ownership of 154 enslaved people**. While he could write movingly about natural rights and express private doubts about slavery, he never took meaningful steps toward emancipation and depended entirely on enslaved labor for his lifestyle.

His **financial management** contradicted his intellectual sophistication. Despite earning substantial income as a lawyer and inheriting significant property, he consistently overspent and left his family in debt. He acknowledged this, writing of his "thraldom of mind" over financial troubles, yet continued expensive building projects and entertaining.

Jefferson preached **republican simplicity** while maintaining an aristocratic lifestyle—importing French wines, building an elaborate mansion, keeping a carriage and four. His "democratic" manners coexisted with decidedly elitist tastes and assumptions about who should govern.

## Evolution of Thinking

Jefferson's political philosophy evolved from **idealistic Revolutionary fervor toward more pragmatic governance**. Early letters show pure theoretical devotion to limited government; presidential correspondence reveals accommodation to federal power when necessary (Louisiana Purchase, embargo policies).

His **religious views shifted** from conventional Anglicanism toward deistic rationalism. Late in life, he compiled his own version of Jesus's teachings, stripping away supernatural elements. He told his daughter he had put his "religious creed on paper" to help family "estimate the libels published against me."

On **slavery**, Jefferson moved from youthful hopes for gradual emancipation toward fatalistic acceptance. Early writings suggest he expected slavery to end; later correspondence shows resignation that it would persist beyond his lifetime.

His **faith in popular democracy** remained remarkably consistent, though tempered by experience. Even after bitter partisan attacks, he maintained that "the people" would ultimately judge correctly.

## Key Relationships and Influences

**George Wythe** was his most formative mentor—Jefferson called him "my faithful and beloved mentor in youth, and my most affectionate friend through life." Wythe's emphasis on systematic learning and moral philosophy profoundly shaped Jefferson's approach to both law and ethics.

**Martha Wayles Jefferson** was clearly his deepest emotional attachment. Her death in 1782 devastated him—he remained in his room three weeks, then rode obsessively alone through the mountains. He promised her never to remarry and kept that promise. The loss of their daughter Maria in 1804 reopened this wound: "Others may lose of their abundance, but I, of my want, have lost even the half of all I had."

**James Madison** became his closest political collaborator and intellectual companion. Their correspondence reveals Jefferson's most sophisticated political thinking. He trusted Madison completely and engineered his succession to the presidency.

**Lafayette** embodied Jefferson's hopes for global republicanism. Their friendship survived Lafayette's imprisonment and Jefferson's presidency, representing Jefferson's enduring faith in revolutionary ideals.

## Beyond the Published Work

Jefferson was **obsessed with architecture and design**—constantly rebuilding Monticello, designing the University of Virginia, sketching improvements. This wasn't mere hobby but intellectual passion for rational space and beauty.

His **love of music** was profound and practical. He played violin daily, imported a forte-piano from Paris, and wrote that "This is the favorite passion of my soul." Music provided emotional outlet his reserved personality otherwise suppressed.

**Gardening** became almost spiritual practice. Granddaughters described his joy planting hyacinths with fancy names ("Marcus Aurelius," "Queen of the Amazons"), his daily walks among flowers, his correspondence with botanists. His garden book, kept for decades, recorded everything from first peach blossoms to wheat harvests.

He was deeply **sentimental about family objects and places**. He kept his wife's prayer book, treasured locks of his daughters' hair, preserved his father's surveying instruments. Monticello itself became a shrine to memory—he couldn't bear to change his wife's room for years after her death.

## How Contemporaries Described Them

**Margaret Bayard Smith** found him surprisingly informal: "His dress was of plain cloth, and he rode on horseback to the Capitol without a single guard or even servant in his train."

**The Duc de la Rochefoucauld-Liancourt** admired his systematic mind: "his superior mind directs the management of his domestic concerns with the same abilities, activity, and regularity which he evinced in the conduct of public affairs."

**John Adams** wrote with complex mixture of admiration and frustration: "Jefferson had a great gift for silence" and possessed "a happy talent of communication, correct and gentlemanly manners."

**A granddaughter** captured his essential quality: "Cheerfulness, love, benevolence, wisdom, seemed to animate his whole form. His face beamed with them." Yet she also noted his reserve: he was "somewhat cold" to strangers though warm with family.

**Political enemies** consistently attacked his **perceived inconsistencies**—southern slaveholder preaching equality, wealthy planter claiming to represent common farmers, sophisticated intellectual posing as simple democrat. These weren't entirely unfair criticisms, though they missed his genuine commitment to republican principles and individual liberty within the constraints he inherited.

The portrait that emerges is of a man of extraordinary intellectual gifts and genuine human warmth, whose idealism was both his greatest strength and the source of his most troubling contradictions. He lived the tensions of his era—between Enlightenment ideals and social realities, between public service and private happiness, between revolutionary hopes and practical governance—more intensely than most of his contemporaries, making him both admirable and deeply human.
